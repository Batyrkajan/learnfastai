Module: Scrum Framework

The Scrum Framework is an agile project management methodology designed to deliver value incrementally through iterative and collaborative processes. It emphasizes flexibility, transparency, and continuous improvement, making it ideal for complex projects.

80/20 Study Guide - Key Concepts

Scrum Roles

Scrum defines three core roles: the Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Development Team, each with distinct responsibilities to ensure project success.

The 20% You Need to Know:

  • Product Owner: Represents stakeholders, prioritizes the product backlog, and ensures value delivery.
  • Scrum Master: Facilitates the Scrum process, removes impediments, and coaches the team.
  • Development Team: Self-organizing group responsible for delivering increments of the product.

Why It Matters:

Clear roles ensure accountability, collaboration, and efficient workflow, enabling teams to adapt quickly to changes.

Simple Takeaway:

Each role has a unique purpose, and their collaboration drives the success of the Scrum process.

Sprints

A Sprint is a time-boxed iteration, typically 2-4 weeks, during which the team delivers a potentially shippable product increment.

The 20% You Need to Know:

  • Sprints are fixed in length and cannot be extended.
  • Each Sprint begins with planning and ends with a review and retrospective.
  • The goal is to deliver a working product increment by the end of the Sprint.

Why It Matters:

Sprints provide a predictable cadence for delivery, enabling teams to adapt to feedback and changing requirements.

Simple Takeaway:

Sprints are the heartbeat of Scrum, ensuring continuous progress and adaptability.

Scrum Artifacts

Scrum artifacts include the Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, and Increment, which provide transparency and track progress.

The 20% You Need to Know:

  • Product Backlog: A prioritized list of features, requirements, and tasks.
  • Sprint Backlog: A subset of the Product Backlog selected for the current Sprint.
  • Increment: The sum of all completed Product Backlog items at the end of a Sprint.

Why It Matters:

Artifacts ensure transparency, alignment, and focus, helping teams deliver value consistently.

Simple Takeaway:

Artifacts are the tools that keep the team aligned and focused on delivering value.

Scrum Events

Scrum events include Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, and Sprint Retrospective, which structure the workflow and foster collaboration.

The 20% You Need to Know:

  • Sprint Planning: Defines the Sprint goal and selects items from the Product Backlog.
  • Daily Scrum: A 15-minute stand-up to synchronize work and identify blockers.
  • Sprint Review: Demonstrates the completed work to stakeholders.
  • Sprint Retrospective: Reflects on the Sprint to identify improvements.

Why It Matters:

Events create a rhythm for the team, ensuring regular communication, feedback, and improvement.

Simple Takeaway:

Scrum events keep the team aligned, focused, and continuously improving.
