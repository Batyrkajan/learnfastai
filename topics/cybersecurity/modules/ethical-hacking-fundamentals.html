Module: Ethical Hacking Fundamentals

This module introduces the core principles of ethical hacking, focusing on understanding cybersecurity threats, vulnerabilities, and the tools and techniques used to protect systems. Ethical hacking is a proactive approach to securing digital assets by simulating attacks to identify and fix weaknesses.

80/20 Study Guide - Key Concepts

What is Ethical Hacking?

Ethical hacking involves authorized attempts to gain unauthorized access to systems, networks, or data to identify vulnerabilities and improve security.

The 20% You Need to Know:

  • Ethical hackers use the same tools and techniques as malicious hackers but with permission.
  • The goal is to identify and fix vulnerabilities before they can be exploited.
  • Ethical hacking is legal and follows a strict code of conduct.

Why It Matters:

Ethical hacking is critical for preventing data breaches, protecting sensitive information, and ensuring compliance with cybersecurity regulations.

Simple Takeaway:

Ethical hacking is like a security checkup for your systems, helping you stay one step ahead of cybercriminals.

Types of Hackers

Hackers are categorized based on their intent and methods: ethical (white hat), malicious (black hat), and those in between (gray hat).

The 20% You Need to Know:

  • White hat hackers work legally to improve security.
  • Black hat hackers exploit vulnerabilities for personal gain or malicious purposes.
  • Gray hat hackers operate in a moral gray area, often without permission but without malicious intent.

Why It Matters:

Understanding the types of hackers helps organizations prepare for different threats and implement appropriate defenses.

Simple Takeaway:

Not all hackers are bad‚Äîethical hackers are the good guys protecting your systems.

Common Cybersecurity Threats

Cybersecurity threats are potential dangers that can exploit vulnerabilities in systems, networks, or data.

The 20% You Need to Know:

  • Malware: Software designed to harm systems or steal data.
  • Phishing: Fraudulent attempts to obtain sensitive information by pretending to be a trustworthy entity.
  • Ransomware: Malware that encrypts data and demands payment for its release.

Why It Matters:

Recognizing common threats helps organizations implement targeted defenses and reduce the risk of attacks.

Simple Takeaway:

Stay vigilant‚Äîcybersecurity threats are constantly evolving, and awareness is your first line of defense.
